I left home at twelve. By sixteen I had a high-school diploma, a five-hundred-dollar Jeep,
and a phone bill I paid myself. None of that was identity. It was repetition — showing up to
the ranch before the sun, planting fence line nobody would thank me for, learning to teach a
horse pace before I had any of my own.

The Air Force gave that repetition a name: the standard. You hold it when no one is watching,
or it isn't a standard. I ran a kitchen at a missile alert facility in the Wyoming cold and
deployed to Kyrgyzstan, and the lesson was the same one the ranch taught — discipline comes
first, and identity is what's left after you've done the work long enough to stop asking who
you are.

That's the throughline into everything since: the production, the tools I build, the books I
write. I document the becoming as it happens — not the polished retrospective, the field
notes of someone still in it.
